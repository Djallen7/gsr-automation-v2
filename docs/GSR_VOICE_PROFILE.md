# GSR Writing Voice Profile

**Show:** Genesis Science Report (GSR), Season 3  
**Host:** David Rives  
**Source:** Synthesized from 323 writing-related conversations (879-session archaeology), production guidelines, script corpus, and iterative revision history  
**Last updated:** 2026-05-30  
**How to use:** Read Part 1 and 4 before any writing session. Jump to the relevant segment section in Part 2 for the specific segment you're writing. Consult Part 3 when the guest topic affects tone. Part 5 is the craft reference. Part 6 is what you reach for when a pattern is starting to feel familiar.

## Part 1: The GSR Voice DNA

### What the show is
A weekly 58-minute Christian creation-science TV program. Biblical worldview informs topic selection and framing — not delivery style. The show sounds like cable news produced by a ministry, not a ministry that borrowed cable news aesthetics. The difference matters in every sentence.

### David Rives — voice characteristics
David is a confident, curious generalist who puts specialists' findings in front of a lay audience. He is not a scientist — he is the viewer's intelligent, scripturally-grounded guide. His register is anchor-desk, not lecture hall.

- **Sentence length:** Short declarative sentences (5–10 words) are the backbone. One payoff sentence per beat (up to 25 words) before returning to short beats. Hard cap: 20 words per teleprompter sentence.
- **Reading level:** 7th grade. Every technical term must be immediately defined in plain language.
- **Vocabulary:** Specific, concrete, named. He names astronauts. He cites the exact article. He gives temperatures and distances. Generic scripts feel wrong because they float above the actual material.
- **Register:** Present-tense, direct-address, contractions. "Well, folks," "You know," "I'll tell you that." The cadence is Fox News declarative — not Fox News aggressive.
- **Tone:** Confident, curious, grounded in Scripture. Never sensational. Never preachy. The biblical application is the landing point, not the launch pad.
- **Authenticity markers:** David uses "You see," as a bridge to explanation. He reflects before escalating: "Think about that." He occasionally speaks as a fellow learner: "As you have heard me say so many times before."

### The GSR register
Every sentence does one of three things: establishes a tension, builds toward its resolution, or releases it. Sentences that only describe — without creating or releasing tension — are candidates for deletion.

The show never lectures. It invites. The viewer is assumed to be intelligent and curious, not ignorant and in need of correction.

## Part 2: Segment-by-Segment Voice Map

Run-of-show order. Segments not requiring scripted live copy (Heavens Declare, Kids Corner video, Genesis Science Q&A video) are noted with their toss guidance only.

### 2.1 Show Intro (~0:30) [RC: B2]

**Function:** Establish anchor credibility, preview the episode.

**Locked opener (verbatim every episode):**
> *"Good evening and welcome to The Genesis Science Report. I'm David Rives, and coming up tonight —"*

> Note: the trailing dash here marks a spoken-cadence pause in a locked verbatim on-air line. It is not written copy, so the project "no em dashes" rule does not apply to it. Do not alter the locked quote.

Everything after that is variable. Write two short interview previews using the news-anchor teaser register: hook first, guest name last, active verbs for the guest's role.

**What varies well:**
> *"Dr. Carl Werner joins us once again to walk through what he's been uncovering — and why those findings continue to challenge long-held assumptions."*

**What signals a stale Show Intro:** Guest name first, passive verbs ("will join us to discuss"), listing the episode topics rather than creating tension about them.

**Closes with:** "That and more, coming up on tonight's show... But first."

---

### 2.2 Opening Monologue (~7 min) [RC: B3]

**Function:** Hook the viewer before they can look away. Preview both interviews. Pivot into the monologue topic.

#### Cold Open
The show intro ends with "But first." The monologue opens cold on its own topic — no recap of what the show just said.

The cold open does NOT start mid-thought. The opener is still David's name and show identification. The hook fires in the **second sentence**, not the first.

**Structure:**
1. Name + show ID: *"Good evening and welcome... I'm David Rives."*
2. Immediate hook (2–3 sentences): A headline, a claim, or a contradiction.
3. Preview both interviews.
4. "But first..." pivot into monologue body.

**The hook pattern (contradiction first):**
> *"Tonight — scientists say they've found Earth's twin, 146 light-years away. The headlines are calling it a breakthrough. The data says it may be colder than the surface of Mars."*

Two sentences that contradict each other. The contradiction is the hook. The third sentence is not needed if the tension is already loaded.

**What is expressly forbidden:** "Hello and welcome." Any warm-up sentence before the hook. Re-stating the show's thesis. Preachy framing.

#### Interview Previews (in the Show Intro block)
Three registers, from weakest to strongest:

**Weakest (early-season pattern, now stale):**
> *"Coming up on tonight's show, we'll sit down for a conversation with [field] expert [name] as we discuss [topic]."*

**Mid-range (Museum Bias rhetorical question style):**
> *"What if the fossils on display at the world's most prestigious museums tell a different story? Dr. Carl Werner takes us inside the Smithsonian..."*

**Strongest (Artemis style — guest answers the tension):**
> *"Tonight — scientists say they've found Earth's twin. Dr. Frank Sherwin takes the discovery apart and tells us what the coverage is leaving out."*

The guest's role is always active: "takes apart," "explains why," "walks us through." Never passive.

**Interview 2 preview** uses "Then later —" as its opener and shifts to shorter, more poetic beats:
> *"Then later — the code inside every living cell. Four letters. Written in a language older than language itself."*

#### Monologue Body
Opens immediately after "But first." Does not recap the previews.

**Structure:**
1. Scene-setter (connects viewer to something familiar — a headline, a school lesson, a space mission)
2. Build (3–5 paragraphs developing the topic)
3. Scripture (1–2 references, never before the midpoint — Scripture is the answer key, not the thesis)
4. Close (creates anticipation for Interview 1)

**Sentence rhythm — triplets and denials:**
- **Triplet:** *"We find order. We find precision. We find the fingerprints of a God who spoke the universe into existence."* — three parallel short sentences, escalating in scale.
- **Denial:** *"Not in a science fiction movie. Not in a simulation. The real deal."* — two beats that establish the category, the third breaks it and lands the point.

**Rhetorical questions:** Appear at transition points between monologue sections, not as openers. Ask and immediately answer.

**Scripture timing:** Psalm 19 territory, Deuteronomy 6, Genesis 1 — these appear in the final third of the monologue body. The scientific narrative runs ~75% of the runtime. Scripture is the landing.

**Direct-address beats:** *"Think about that."* / *"Well, folks."* / *"You see,"* — these are the breathing points. Use them before a payoff sentence or after a dense factual sequence.

### 2.3 Pre-Break Tease — Interview 1 [RC: B11 tease]

**Function:** Give the viewer a reason to come back from the break. The "so what" must be present.

**Structure:** Tension or incongruity first → guest name last → closer.

**The three approved closers (rotate):**
- *"Stay with us."* (preferred for Interview 1)
- *"We'll be right back."*
- *"Don't go anywhere."*

Never "Stay tuned" alone.

**Working examples:**
> *"Darwin described the cell as a blob of protoplasm. Modern biology describes it as a factory. Philosopher of science Dr. Stephen Meyer joins us in-studio on what changed, and what it means. Stay with us."*

> *"A new claim says soil takes centuries to form — but can be destroyed in minutes. That tension raises a bigger question about how quickly the Earth's surface can actually change. Glenn Wilson joins us to examine what the evidence shows. We'll be right back."*

> *"Biology's most fundamental rule says the genetic code reads one way. A Berkeley research team just found an organism that reads it two ways. The microbe is thriving anyway. Microbiologist Dr. Andrew Fabich joins us to explain what they actually found. Stay with us."*

**Guest name lands last** — always. The tension is named before the guest who resolves it.

**The Failure #4 pattern** (documented, widely cited): Previewing the whole interview — naming the topics, the subtopics, the conclusion. When the viewer comes back, there's nowhere for the interview to go. **Rule: give the incongruity. Do not give the topic list.**

**Kill list:** "fascinating," "stay tuned" alone, "a look at," "details ahead," "Up next" or "Coming up" as standalone openers.

### 2.4 Interview 1 — Live Segment (~12 min) [RC: C2]

#### Post-Break Welcome
> *"Welcome back to the Report."* — then immediately into the host intro. No re-orientation, no segment identifier.

#### Host Intro (~45–75 seconds)
Opens on the topic, not the guest. Guest name comes after the hook. The hook must carry weight as the first content post-break.

**The approved model:**
> *"A river that cuts straight through a mountain range. Not around it. Through it."*  
> *"There's a bacterium living in the soil outside your window right now that also lives in Patagonia, in Antarctica, and in a rice paddy in Vietnam. Same organism. Every continent on Earth."*

Pattern: concrete image → violation of expectation → no setup sentence needed. The image does the work.

**What the host intro holds back:** The article's conclusion. The guest will provide it. The intro establishes the tension only.

#### Guest Bio
Immediately follows the host intro. One sentence. Formula: `[Name] is [title] at/with [affiliation], and [one specific thing they've done or found relevant to tonight's topic].`

**Never read the guest's own bio verbatim.** Rewrite it so it frames their relevance to tonight's segment. If the guest is from Discovery Institute, BioLogos, or a similar institution with a mixed audience, lead science-first; let the worldview connection come from the guest.

#### Question Arc — 4 sections
The arc exists to create a narrative, not a quiz.

**Section 1 — Opening Hook (2 questions):** Draw the viewer in with the most counter-intuitive finding. "What exactly did you find, and why should our viewers pay attention to this?" The guest answers before most viewers know what the segment is about.

**Section 2 — Discovery/Data (3–4 questions):** Walk through what was found, how it was found, and why it matters. One idea per question. No compound questions (anything with "and" connecting two ideas — split them or drop one). Never yes/no answerable in one word. Technical terms must be prompted for definition.

**Section 3 — Biblical Connection (2–3 questions):** *"What does this mean for how we understand the creation account in Genesis?"* The framing rule: **Do not force this.** Let the guest lead. If the guest is secular, omit Section 3 entirely.

**Section 4 — Worldview / Takeaway (2 questions):** First question challenges mainstream science's position. Final question always: *"What is the one thing you most want our viewers to take away from this?"*

David uses the guest's first name after the first question. He never asks about YouTube, social media, or where to follow them.

#### Interview Outro
One sentence of specific personal praise → tie to a resource at creationsuperstore.com → CTA as the closing line.

> *"If you want to see Joe do what he does best — taking the camera into the field and letting the geological record speak for itself — his documentary FIRE and ICE follows him to Iceland to examine the climate and fossil record firsthand. You can pick it up at creationsuperstore.com."*

If no product on the store: website CTA instead.

### 2.5 THD (Heavens Declare) Toss [RC: C8]

**Function:** Hand off to pre-produced video. Name the segment, introduce a tension, close with the canonical line.

**Canonical closer (locked, verbatim):** *"Let's take a look, right now."*

**Target length:** 3 sentences. Maximum: 6.

**Structure:** Question or tension → segment named explicitly → canonical closer.

**Working examples:**
> *"How do we recognize information when we see it? Whether it's a written message, spoken words, or a coded system, language always points back to a mind behind it. In this week's Heavens Declare, we're examining one of the most complex information systems in the natural world. Let's take a look, right now."*

> *"Next, we turn our attention to the natural world — and the message it's sending. On this week's Heavens Declare, we explore whether nature speaks for itself — or if something greater is speaking through it. Let's take a look, right now."*

**When a natural link to the preceding interview exists:** Use it. When it doesn't: skip the bridge. Forced thematic connections are the most-cited THD failure in the archive. A smooth gear-shift beats a strained metaphor.

**What fails:** Recapping the preceding interview. Generic "let's take a look at creation" without specific tension. Repeating the same curiosity device used in the GSM toss earlier in the episode (these are written together — they must diverge).

### 2.6 Kids Corner / Q&A Block [RC: D2]

**Post-break structure:** Q&A is previewed briefly first (even though KC airs first), because this lets David say "but first" — a micro-hook before the roll-in. KC is named second.

**Working example:**
> *"Welcome back. In just a moment, our Genesis Science Q&A takes us right here to the Wonders Center, where David answers a question about how his love for science began. But first, it's time for this week's Kids Corner, where we're taking a close-up look at amber, fossilized tree sap, and the tiny creatures sometimes trapped inside. Let's jump in."*

**Kids Corner closer:** *"Let's jump in."* or *"Here's this week's Kids Corner."* — lighter register than THD/GSM. Not the same canonical closer.

**Q&A mention:** Brief or omitted. Only include if the Q&A topic is genuinely compelling. The archive shows it appears in the toss ~3 of 5 episodes.

**Universal anchor for Kids Corner:** Something any viewer already recognizes — amber, Jurassic Park, a spider in tree sap. Curiosity, not urgency. Children are in the audience.

### 2.7 Ministry Report (~2 min) [RC: E2]

**Function:** Ministry announcements, events, donation pitch.

**Canonical opener:** *"Welcome back. It's time for this week's Ministry Report..."*

**Register:** The warmest segment in the show. First-person plural, community-facing, announcement cadence — not promotional. Think: a pastor in an announcement slot, not a nonprofit development pitch.

**Structure:** Place-rooted or time-rooted opener → ministry news in descending specificity (event/venue first, then media, then donation close) → donation CTA as the final sentence.

**Donation close (never cold — always wrapped in gratitude):**
> *"If you'd like to partner with us, you can make a tax-deductible gift by visiting our website or calling 931-212-7990."*

The phone number is always the last sentence. It never drops cold. The "grateful" framing comes first.

**Event announcements:** Concrete anchor (day, activity, audience). Specifics over hype. One clear reason to show up. Let David's credibility carry it.

**What fails:** Report-like structure (fact after fact). Fundraising copy language. Any language that makes the audience feel like a donor pool rather than a community.

### 2.8 Viewer Voices (~2 min) [RC: E4]

**Current Season 3 structure:** David does NOT read testimonials from the anchor desk. He tosses to a staff reporter (Miryam) in a live-hit room. David's spoken part is a two-sentence maximum functional handoff.

**David's toss (functional, not promotional):**
> *"Every week, we hear from viewers all over the country — people who've watched an episode, visited the museum, or pulled a book off our shelves and taken something useful away from it. Miryam, what do you have for us this week?"*

**What was explicitly rejected:** *"We love hearing from viewers who are watching and digging deeper."* — flagged as insincere praise with no anchor.

**Four fixed toss lines** rotate weekly. David's job: hand off. He does not sell the segment.

**Reporter format (Miryam):** "Hey David" → reads testimonial → two-sentence authentic response → CTA (comments@genesissciencenetwork.com) → "David, back to you."

### 2.9 Featured Resource (~2 min) [RC: E6]

**Function:** Promote a book or product at creationsuperstore.com. Treated as an editorial recommendation, not a store announcement.

**David's portion (1–3 sentences):** Curiosity hook or visual intrigue → toss to bookstore host.
> *"Some of the most mysterious creatures ever discovered didn't live on land... they lived beneath the surface of the ocean. Let's head over to the Creation Superstore."*

Stakes → toss. Not: product name → description → buy now.

**Bookstore host format:** Show-and-react opening ("Take a look at this") → what the book covers in plain terms → who it's for → URL close.

**Hard rule:** `creationsuperstore.com` must be spoken on-air. The segment closes with the URL.

**What fails:** Language that "feels commercial." Product-first rather than curiosity-first. Anything that makes David sound like an announcer rather than a trusted source.

### 2.10 GSM (Genesis Science Minute) Toss [RC: E8]

**Function:** Same as THD toss. Different enough in device that the two don't feel like copies of each other within the same episode.

**Canonical closer (same as THD):** *"Let's take a look, right now."*

**Minimum length:** 3 sentences.

**The key discipline:** The GSM toss and the THD toss are produced in the same session. They must use different rhetorical devices. If THD used a question-drop, GSM uses an everyday moment. If THD used incongruity, GSM uses a concrete image. Never the same opener type in both.

**Working examples:**
> *"The next time you reach for a Band-Aid, take a second to think about what your body is doing underneath the skin. It's a process most of us take for granted. But the design behind it is anything but ordinary. This week's Genesis Science Minute. Let's take a look, right now."*

> *"There's a fish that was supposed to have been extinct for seventy million years. In 1938, a fisherman caught one in his net off the coast of South Africa — alive. And that fish is not alone. Here's this week's Genesis Science Minute."*

The "everyday moment" pivot and the "supposed to be extinct" pattern are both strong openers — neither requires the segment name before the closer.

Same rules as Interview 1 tease. Slight variation in closer preference — "We'll be right back" and "Don't go anywhere" are slightly preferred for Interview 2 as the harder toss.

The second tease must not reuse the tension type from the first tease. If Interview 1 tease used an anomaly (organism that reads genetic code two ways), Interview 2 tease should use a different entry point (scale, stakes, contradiction with mainstream claim, everyday-object pivot).

### 2.13 Closing (~1 min) [RC: F8]

**Locked sign-off (verbatim — do not alter):**
> *"That's it for today. Thank you for joining us on the Genesis Science Report. Until next week, keep looking up. I'm David Rives. Truly, the heavens declare the glory of God."*

Register: liturgical. This is the show's benediction. Nothing before it should feel like padding. If there is pre-close copy, it is one sentence of episode summary or upcoming episode preview, then the sign-off.

## Part 3: Contextual Adaptation by Topic Domain

The host intro, pre-break tease, and monologue body all adapt to the domain of the guest or topic. Here are the contextual signals and the writing approach each triggers:

### Geology / Flood / Catastrophism (Clarey, Austin, Whitmore)
**Tension:** Time-scale disruption. How fast can the Earth actually change?  
**Hook device:** Landscape or process that violates the assumed timescale. *"A river that cuts straight through a mountain range."*  
**Scripture territory:** Genesis flood account, Psalm 104.  
**Tone:** Investigative. The geology makes the case; David guides the viewer through it.

### Genetics / Cell Biology / Biochemistry (Fabich, DeWitt, Tomkins, Behe)
**Tension:** The ordinary made strange. Precision and ubiquity as the hook.  
**Hook device:** Something that happens inside the viewer's body right now, made specific. *"Right now, inside your body, bone marrow is building two million red blood cells every second, with atoms placed in exact sequence."*  
**Scripture territory:** Psalm 139, Genesis 1 (kinds).  
**Tone:** Wonder-driven. The complexity of the ordinary is the argument.

### Astronomy / Cosmology (Lisle, Faulkner, Hebert, Psarris)
**Tension:** The scale of creation vs. the precision required for life.  
**Hook device:** A specific number, mission, or discovery — named, dated, specific. *"146 light-years away. The headlines are calling it a breakthrough."*  
**Scripture territory:** Psalm 19, Psalm 8, Job 38.  
**Tone:** News-driven plus wonder. Engineering specifics → awe at the Creator's scale.

### Archaeology / Biblical History (Stripling, Ross, Nelson, Herman)
**Tension:** Mainstream historiography vs. the biblical record — and when the ground speaks for itself.  
**Hook device:** A specific site, artifact, or excavation that confirms Scripture. The "verification of the historical record" frame.  
**Scripture territory:** Exodus narrative, Genesis genealogies, Acts 17.  
**Tone:** Narrative / storytelling cadence. More atmospheric. Pop culture references (Ten Commandments, Indiana Jones) are welcome as entry points.

### Physics / Cosmology / Young-Universe (Humphreys, Hartnett, Houts)
**Tension:** Mainstream claim vs. the actual data.  
**Hook device:** The anomaly. What the mainstream says vs. what the evidence shows.  
**Scripture territory:** Genesis 1 (creation days), Job 38.  
**Tone:** More combative framing against mainstream claims. *"Despite what mainstream science claims..."* is acceptable here.

### Human Origins / Evolution Critique (DeWitt, Oard, Sanford)
**Tension:** What's being taught in schools vs. what the evidence supports.  
**Hook device:** Frame through the parent-and-family audience. *"Whether the evidence still supports the evolutionary timeline that's being taught in our schools."*  
**Scripture territory:** Genesis 1–2, Romans 5 (Adam as historical figure).  
**Tone:** Accessible to a family audience. Not academic.

### Non-YEC / Secular Scientists (Barentine, Dodson, Schweitzer)
**Adjustment:** Keep biblical worldview framing only in David's copy — not directed at the guest. Science-first throughout the interview. Section 3 (Biblical Connection) of the question arc is omitted. Let the science make the case; David applies the worldview in his outro if at all.

## Part 4: Anti-Patterns — What Makes It Stale

### The Kill List
These phrases appear in the archive's explicit rejection record. Their presence in a first draft is a signal to rewrite the paragraph, not just the phrase:
- "fascinating"
- "Stay tuned" (as a standalone)
- "A look at" (as a segment preview opener)
- "Details ahead"
- "I hope this email finds you well" — and its on-air equivalents: "We love hearing from you," "Thank you so much for being with us"
- "Up next" or "Coming up" as standalone openers with no hook
- Any guest name-first tease or preview

### The Three-Short-Beats Trap
The triplet rhythm is one of David's strongest techniques. It's also the most over-deployed. When three short declarative sentences appear in a set with no variation in length or type, and then appear again two paragraphs later, and again in the next segment's toss — the viewer clocks the pattern before the content.

**Rule:** Use triplets strategically, not as default. One per script block (monologue body, interview toss, THD toss) is enough.

### Statement-Driven Hooks
The default AI failure mode on GSR copy: leading with the fact rather than the tension. *"Scientists have discovered a new exoplanet 146 light-years from Earth."* is a statement. *"Scientists say they've found Earth's twin. The data says it may be colder than the surface of Mars."* is a tension.

Every host intro and tease should be audited: is the first line a statement or a tension?

### The Topic-List Tease
Previewing the interview topic list: *"Coming up — we'll discuss coded information, miniature machines, and the level of engineering in the cell."* The viewer has been briefed. When they come back from break, there's nothing to discover. **Give the incongruity. Never give the topic list.**

### Forced Thematic Bridges in Tosses
Connecting the preceding interview to the next segment's topic when the link isn't genuinely there. The archive calls this out specifically for THD tosses. A smooth gear-change is better than a strained metaphor.

### Pastoral Warmth in Newsy Segments
The Ministry Report has a warm register. That warmth should not bleed into the opening monologue, interview tosses, or pre-break teases. If a segment outside the Ministry Report block starts using "we're so grateful," "it's been such an encouragement," or similar community-building language, it's a register mismatch.

### Generic Praise in Viewer Voices and Interview Outros
"One of the most encouraging parts of this work" — no anchor, no tension, nothing earned. Specific praise only. What did the guest actually do? What did the viewer actually say? The specificity is what makes it feel genuine.

## Part 5: Literary Techniques

### 1. Contradiction Opener
Two consecutive sentences that contradict each other. The contradiction IS the hook. No setup needed.
> *"The headlines are calling it a breakthrough. The data says it may be colder than the surface of Mars."*

### 2. Triplet / Escalating Parallel
Three short sentences of the same structure, escalating in scale on the third.
> *"We find order. We find precision. We find the fingerprints of a God who spoke the universe into existence."*

### 3. Denial Pattern (Not X / Not Y / Real Thing)
Two beats that establish a category, the third breaks it and lands the point.
> *"Not in a science fiction movie. Not in a simulation. The real deal."*

### 4. Universal Anchor → Disruption → Stakes
The approved structure for hooks that must work on non-specialist viewers (Kids Corner, opening monologue scene-setter, Featured Resource):
1. Anchor the viewer in something familiar they already own (a Band-Aid, a river they've seen, a space mission in the news)
2. Disrupt their assumption about it
3. Name the stakes

### 5. Question-Drop (for THD / GSM tosses and transition moments)
Ask one question — do not answer it — then name who or what will answer it. The question must be one the viewer genuinely cannot answer from their existing knowledge.
> *"How do we recognize information when we see it?"*

### 6. Active Guest Role
The guest is always doing something: "takes apart," "explains why," "walks us through," "shows us what's been left out." Never passive. Never "will join us to discuss."

### 7. Specificity as Credibility
Named astronauts, exact temperatures, article titles, site coordinates. Specificity signals research. Vagueness signals AI default. One specific named detail per segment block is the floor.

### 8. Beat-and-Reflect
After a dense factual sequence, one short direct-address sentence before the next beat.
> *"Think about that."* / *"Well, folks."* / *"You see,"*

These are breathing points. They invite the viewer to sit with the information before moving forward.

### 9. Withhold the Resolution
The host intro does not state the article's conclusion. The tease does not list the interview topics. The GSM toss does not summarize the video. The tension is loaded; the content delivers the release.

## Part 6: The Variation Framework — How to Keep It Alive

Writing three variations of a segment does not mean three ways to say the same thing. It means three different rhetorical entry points for the same material. The viewer is implicitly asking a different question in each version.

### The Five Rhetorical Modes
Pick one per segment. Do not blend. Do not reuse the same mode across two tosses or two teases in the same episode.

**Breaking News / Headline-Driven**  
> Entry point: What just happened? Register: present-tense urgency, compressed sentences, named specifics.  
> Best for: Interview teases on current research, pre-break hooks, astronomy/cosmology topics.

**Wonder-Driven**  
> Entry point: What is strange and beautiful about this? Register: slower pace, more expansive sentences, leads with awe before argument.  
> Best for: Genetics/cell biology intros, THD tosses, closing monologue beats.

**Stakes-Focused**  
> Entry point: What does this mean for the viewer's life, family, or faith? Register: second-person address, parent/family framing, consequences foregrounded.  
> Best for: Human origins topics, education/schools framing, featured resource.

**Curiosity-Driven**  
> Entry point: What is something the viewer doesn't know they don't know? Register: question-drop, everyday object pivot, understatement.  
> Best for: Kids Corner, GSM tosses, Featured Resource teaser.

**Narrative / Investigative**  
> Entry point: There's a story here — follow me. Register: storytelling cadence, named characters and places, slower build.  
> Best for: Archaeology topics, documentary-style guest intros (Werner, Hubbard), history-of-science angles.

### The Rotation Rule
Across any single episode, no two segments in the same structural slot should use the same rhetorical mode. The two interview teases must diverge. The THD and GSM tosses must diverge. The monologue body and the interview host intro should use different entry points if the topics allow it.

### What Evolution Looks Like
The voice profile is a calibration tool, not a template. Good variation is new rhetorical angles, not new words for the same angle. If a technique has appeared in the last 3 episodes, consider whether a different mode serves the material better.

## Part 7: AI Session Rules

These are the documented failure modes from 323 writing-related conversations. Apply them as session-start guardrails.

### Documented failure modes (all observed in production):

1. **Statement-driven hooks** — AI defaults to stating facts. GSR requires tension. Audit every first line.
2. **Three-short-beats overuse** — AI falls back on triplet rhythm across all segments in a session, making the episode sound rhythmically uniform. Break it deliberately.
3. **Multiple options when one was requested** — Produces 3–5 alternatives instead of a single strong deliverable. This is friction for a solo producer under deadline. **When Daniel asks for one, write one.**
4. **Long explanation before producing copy** — *"Don't over-explain before producing copy. Long visible reasoning frustrates him under deadline."*
5. **Fabricated or unverified credentials** — Never pull guest credentials from secondary summaries or web context. Use only what has been provided. Every credential requires a traceable source.
6. **Context contamination** — In long sessions with multiple uploaded files, AI outputs prior episode scripts rather than new copy. This is the most disruptive failure mode. Verify that the output addresses the current request.
7. **Repeating the same toss device twice in one episode** — THD and GSM tosses, or Interview 1 and Interview 2 teases, default to the same opener type. Explicitly diversify.
8. **Character count drift on lower thirds** — L3 copy overshoots on first pass. Target is 60–65 characters. Push back to length; do not accept short output.
9. **Pastoral warmth outside the Ministry Report** — The warm register bleeds into newsy segments. The ministry relationship between David and the audience is implied, not stated, in every segment except the Ministry Report.
10. **Topic-list teases** — Listing interview subtopics rather than building tension. One incongruity, not three topics.

### Session-start injection
For any writing session, inject:
- The episode's guest names, topics, and article titles (specificity is credibility)
- Which segments are needed (do not over-produce)
- Which rhetorical modes have been used in recent episodes (to drive rotation)
- The `/toolkit` page guest roster (for research sessions — prevents suggesting recently-aired guests)
