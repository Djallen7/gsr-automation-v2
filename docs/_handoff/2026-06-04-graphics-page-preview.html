1Welcome back to The Genesis Science Report.

Water takes the path of least resistance. That's the rule. Rain runs down the easiest slope. 2Rivers bend around hills. Streams cut through the soft rock and find their way around the hard stuff. Every river on every continent behaves that way, except for one stretch of one river in the American West.

The Green River 3carves a canyon 700 meters deep across the Uinta Mountains. Not around. Through. The hardest possible path, across rock that was already there long before the water arrived. Geologists have called it a mystery since John Wesley Powell first wrote about it in 1869.

Last month, 4a team out of the University of Glasgow says they've finally solved it. They're calling the mechanism lithospheric dripping. The mountains sagged, the river cut its path, then the mountains rose back up.

There's a simpler explanation. And tonight's guest has spent twenty years building 5the framework for it. Dr. Tim Clarey is a research scientist at the Institute for Creation Research, 6author of Carved in Stone, and one of the architects of the megasequence model of Flood geology. He joins us from Dallas. Tim, welcome back to the show.
