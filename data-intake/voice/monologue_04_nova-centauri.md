Show 4 Monologue: A New Star in Centaurus

Sir William Herschel, who discovered the planet of Uranus said this: "The undevout astronomer must be mad."

Let me put that in simple, modern English. If you're looking at the heavens for a living, and don't conclude that there is a God, you're CRAZY!

The extraordinary things that happen in the heavens are just so grand, and make us feel so small in comparison, this is just such an easy on-ramp to believing in the glory and power of God.

That brings us to today's topic. In late September of 2025, people looking up in the southern sky noticed something new.

A bright point of light appeared in the constellation Centaurus—a place of stars that has long drawn the attention of stargazers. Centaurus itself is an ancient constellation in the southern sky. It's home to Alpha Centauri, the closest star system to our own.

For a few nights, what astronomers are calling NOVA Centauri 2025 shone bright enough to be seen without a telescope. Then it slowly faded.

This event is what happens when a small, dense star called a white dwarf pulls gas from a nearby companion star. That gas builds up until it ignites in a sudden burst of energy—an explosion so bright it can be seen from ACROSS the galaxy.

Here are 5 interesting facts about Nova Centauri.

This was found with a regular camera! An Australian, Dr. John Seach, discovered it using just a normal DSLR camera in his backyard. And get this… He actually found TWO novas in two nights!

You could actually see this event WITHOUT a telescope. At its brightest (late September 2025), it was bright enough to spot with your naked eyes in the Southern Hemisphere.

It turned PINK. As it faded, the exploding star glowed bright pink/red because of hot hydrogen gas.

This all took place right next to the closest star to Earth. It popped up only about 2 degrees away from Alpha Centauri, the nearest star system to our Sun.

This is understood to be a star that won't stop exploding. It's a dead star (white dwarf) stealing gas from its neighbor. When it steals too much, BOOM! It explodes, gets a million times brighter, then calms down again for a while.

But fun facts aside, we know the Bible tells us that God uses the heavens to speak to us, that's

"And God said, Let there be lights in the firmament of the heaven to divide the day from the night; and let them be for signs, and for seasons, and for days, and years." (Genesis 1:14 KJV)

When we see a new NOVA, it's not random, it's STILL using the laws and physics that were implemented in the beginning. It's one more glimpse into the power and precision of our amazing God.

Like when Balaam spoke of a star yet to come:

"There shall come a Star out of Jacob, and a Sceptre shall rise out of Israel." (Numbers 24:17 KJV)

Every light in the sky—every nova, every shining star—points us back to Him. The universe is not cold or chaotic. It is ordered, designed, and filled with meaning.

So when a nova reveals itself, we can look up and remember: The same God who spoke those stars into existence still rules the heavens today.

This is what Johannes Kepler is said to have expressed when discovering God through scientific study. "O God, I am thinking Thy thoughts after Thee."

It never gets old, the way God is seen in the midst of His creation, and Nova Centauri is just the latest in a line stretching back to ancient times. God has been, and is still speaking today.
