Show 3 Monologue

In January 2026, we received an incredible update to the Cascadia episode we released this last year on the risks attached to the Cascadia Range in the Pacific Northwest.

Seismologists dropped a BOMBSELL that's rattling the West Coast and beyond.

Using swarms of tiny, almost imperceptible earthquakes, researchers from the USGS, UC Davis, and CU Boulder have uncovered a hidden fragment of an ancient tectonic plate lurking where the San Andreas Fault meets the Cascadia Subduction Zone.

According to this research, this "Pioneer Fragment," a remnant of the long-lost Farallon Plate, is stuck to the bottom of the Pacific Plate and sliding northwest beneath the continent.

Visualize the scene at the Mendocino Triple Junction, off Northern California's coast—a chaotic crossroads where three massive plates collide: the Pacific, North American, and Gorda. For years, models painted this as a straightforward grind, with the Gorda Plate diving under North America in subduction, while the San Andreas slices sideways. But these micro-quakes—thousands of them, too small to feel but packed with data—reveal a messier reality.

A chunk has snapped off the North American Plate and is being dragged down with the sinking Gorda. South of that, the Pacific Plate is hauling this Pioneer blob horizontally under the continent, creating hidden faults invisible from the surface. As one researcher noted, it's like gum stuck to a shoe—attached and causing unexpected friction.

This find hits like a seismic wave because it shreds existing earthquake forecasts. The 1992 magnitude 7.2 Mendocino quake? Likely sparked by this fragment.

Now, experts warn it amps up the odds of a megaquake—magnitude 9 or higher—rippling from Cascadia down to the San Andreas, threatening millions along the Pacific Northwest and California.

"It makes possible interaction between the Cascadia and the San Andreas Fault system more likely," said a UC Berkeley seismologist Roland Burgman.

We're talking about a complete OVERHAUL of risk maps, where what we thought was solid ground turns out to be volatile.

It's a stark reminder that our planet's crust isn't the stable foundation we assume; it's dynamic, restless, and full of surprises.

This discovery underscores a world engineered with power and purpose. The Bible captures this in Psalm 104:5: "Who laid the foundations of the earth, that it should not be removed for ever."

God's voice set boundaries for the seas and shaped the land in dramatic fashion, not gradual whim. Yet amid the warnings, there's awe in the design. This fragment's placement—precisely where faults converge—highlights a system balanced for life, even as it shifts. It challenges us to see the earth's power as a testament to divine engineering.

God is the Architect who forged the plates from the beginning, as Genesis 1 declares He separated the dry land from the waters and He is the sustainer, holding these massive forces in check. Without His word upholding them, these fragments could unravel everything.
