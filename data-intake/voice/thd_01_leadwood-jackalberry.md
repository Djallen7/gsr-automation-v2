(On location savannah / Bushveld setting, trees visible in background)
When most people think of Africa, they picture wide open plains… elephants moving in the distance, giraffes above the trees, maybe a lion resting in the grass. This landscape is called a savannah—and it's actually one of the most widespread ecosystems on Earth, covering about twelve percent of the world's land surface.

But there's a particular kind of savannah found in southern Africa known as the Bushveld. 

It's a slightly elevated region—around 2,500 to 4,000 feet above sea level—filled with tall grasses, scattered trees, and some of the most iconic animals on the planet. Elephants, rhinos, cheetahs, hyenas… they all call this place home.

But here's something that often gets overlooked.

It's not just the animals that make this ecosystem work—it's the plants.

(gesture toward nearby tree or pick up a fallen branch)

Take the leadwood tree, for example. As the name suggests, this wood is incredibly dense—so dense, it actually sinks in water. It's one of the hardest woods in Africa, burns very slowly, and even after the tree dies, it can stand for hundreds of years. That makes it incredibly valuable—not just for people, but for the entire ecosystem.

Its leaves feed animals like elephants, giraffes, and impalas. And interestingly, even its ash has been used for things like toothpaste or whitewash.

Now nearby, you'll often find another fascinating tree—the jackalberry.

These trees can grow quite tall, sometimes reaching up to eighty feet, and they produce small, fleshy fruits that a wide range of animals depend on. Warthogs, baboons, birds… even jackals—the tree's namesake—all feed on its fruit.

( mimic holding fruit)

And it doesn't stop there.

The leaves are eaten by elephants and buffalo, while insects like caterpillars depend on them as well. Even people have found uses for this tree—its wood for furniture and canoes, and its bark and roots for traditional remedies.

But one of the most fascinating things about the jackalberry… is where it grows.

(gesture downward)

These trees are often found on termite mounds.

The termites aerate the soil, making it easier for the tree to grow. And in return, the tree provides protection and structure for the termite colony.

It's a relationship that works… because it was designed to.

In a place that many people think of as harsh or barren…

there's actually a deeply connected system of life.

From the largest animals… to the smallest insects… to the trees that support them all.

And it reminds us of something simple, but powerful.

Everything God has made—plants, animals, entire ecosystems—reflects His wisdom, His provision, and His care.

All we have to do… is look
