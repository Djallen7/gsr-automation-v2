## Claude

Two things you've got right, then the one I'd change.

Right: solving this on the front end instead of live is the correct call, and your reason is the real reason. Fixing the dashboard mid-shoot means crawling back through a Claude Code session and reworking it, which is far worse than just typing a correction into a chat. That asymmetry alone justifies spending the night.

What I'd change: repetition by itself won't buy you durability. A loop only teaches the system something if each run gets graded against a known right answer, and you already have that answer. It's the lower thirds that actually aired. So the job isn't "do it again." It's: run against a different past script, compare to the aired version, log every miss, fix the parse, re-run. And the trap to avoid is letting it self-improve unsupervised till morning, because then it overfits to your fifteen or so old scripts. It'll ace those while getting more brittle on the next new format, which is the exact opposite of the versatility you're chasing. So it needs a leash, and I'll come back to that.

One cheap win, since you're the one creating the inputs: give the parser a single fixed anchor. One consistent header line above the lower thirds, same words every time. Stay loose with everything else. That one habit costs you nothing at script-writing time and removes the hardest part of the parse, which is finding where the script ends and the lower thirds begin. Far more reliable than asking it to guess that boundary cold.

Last thing, then a question. What you described is really two pipelines, not one. Lower thirds parsing has a clean answer key. Graphics idea generation is fuzzy and needs the sub-agent critique. Different tests, and I'd build and run them one at a time rather than blended.

So where do we start: designing the lower thirds harness, or pinning down what counts as a "pass" first, since both pipelines hang on that definition?

## Claude

Stop — what you just described is a different and much bigger project than what tonight's session can deliver, and the most useful thing I can do is name that line clearly before you sleep.

The honest verdict: the email-watching, status-anticipating, proactive-nudging "brain" is real and worth building, but it is months of work, not tonight. If you point an unsupervised overnight session at that vision, you wake up to a sprawling half-built mess that touches your email, your Supabase, and your folders, and you trust it less, not more. That is the opposite of what you want.

Here's the distinction that matters. Tonight's job is the parser — script and lower thirds ingestion, getting the graphics table off zero. That has a known right answer and can be tested safely against past scripts. The "brain" — reading guest emails, firing status changes, nudging you to enter a name on confirmation — has no clean answer key, touches live systems, and needs you awake and watching the first hundred times before you'd trust it. Those cannot share an overnight run. One is testable in a sandbox; the other is exactly the thing you said you don't trust to run silently.

Two corrections to your own framing, because you asked me to scrutinize:

You said staying on track comes from anticipating the next step, not silently keeping track. Right instinct. But the anticipation engine is the hardest, highest-risk part of the whole system, and it's the part you most need to supervise live. So it's last to build, not something to hand a sleeping session.

The urgent-items-that-are-three-weeks-stale problem you ended on — that's not a feature you add later. That's evidence that proactive automation without a human check decays into noise fast. It's an argument for building the brain slowly with you in the loop, which reinforces the split.

So my recommendation, high confidence: tonight is the parser, alone, in a sandbox, on a leash. The guest-data and email-watching brain gets scoped as its own project later, built incrementally while you're awake. Don't blend them.

Now the real question for tonight, and it's a blocker. You flagged that your past scripts aren't standardized, don't have the three-variation format, and aren't all fifteen lines. That's actually fine for testing the parser — the parser's job is to survive messy input, so messy past scripts are the perfect test set. But the wipe-and-reassign idea you raised, where it strips graphic cues, regenerates, then looks at the originals again, is a separate capability from parsing. Are you trying to test both tonight, or is tonight strictly: can it reliably read a messy script and populate the tracker and rundown correctly on the first try?
