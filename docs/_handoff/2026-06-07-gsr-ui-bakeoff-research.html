GSR Dashboard UI, Research Highlights

Fresh-research bake-off. 7 teams, ~50 specialized agents, no prior visual assumptions. Generated 2026-06-07.

The one big finding. Seven independent directions, researched separately, all landed on the same information architecture: a short per-role "what needs me" list as the entry point, plus a compact way to see all 10 episodes across the 9 stages at once. They differ in skin, not in structure. So this choice is about the feel and credibility you want, not about how the tool works. That part is settled.

The 7 directions

1. Calm Minimalism

Linear / Things / Vercel. Restraint, one accent, type-led, generous space.

Insight: hierarchy comes from type weight and whitespace, not color or boxes; reserve a single accent for the one thing that needs action. "Structure felt, not seen."

Strengths: calmest possible read for an overwhelmed owner; phone-native; cheap and accessible. Risks: can look "empty/is it broken", and can under-signal urgency unless the at-risk state is allowed to break the calm. Best for: Daniel's glance-and-go overview.

Sources: Linear design refresh, Vercel Geist, Things 3, Stripe color, Calm Technology (Amber Case).

2. Editorial Data-Density

Bloomberg Terminal / Notion / Airtable. Dense made elegant, a real status matrix.

Insight: 10 episodes x 9 stages is a table, so render it as one elegant matrix (glyph + color per cell), not a wall of cards. Tabular numerals, one accent for trouble, hairlines not boxes. Value density over visual density.

Strengths: the whole batch in one glance, scales as episodes change, the producer's mental model. Risks: hostile to phones unless it collapses to cards; can read as "spreadsheet/work". Best for: Daniel on desktop/tablet for the Monday batch review.

Sources: Bloomberg UX, FT Visual Vocabulary, Stripe, Airtable, tabular-figures research.

3. Material 3 Expressive

Google's current system. Tonal color, rounded cards, accessible, mobile-native.

Insight: color generated from one brand seed (auto-accessible light/dark), tonal elevation instead of shadows, large touch targets, status as chips. Field-proven on phones (46 studies, 18k participants).

Strengths: mobile-native, accessible by default, familiar (feels like the Google tools your team already uses). Risks: can look generic/"Google-y" unless re-seeded with a brand color; expressive bounce can feel toy-like for a high-stakes tool. Best for: lowest-risk, fastest-to-trust on a phone.

Sources: m3.material.io (color/type/nav/motion), Android M3 Expressive launch, 9to5Google.

4. Neo-Brutalist Utility (warmed)

Thick borders, flat status blocks, bold type. Warmed for a ministry.

Insight: bold, flat, high-contrast blocks are unusually legible (a WCAG win if linted), and the "physical button" press is unmistakable on a phone. Status triple-encoded (color + glyph + caps).

Strengths: zero ambiguity, distinctive, hard to mis-tap. Risks: can feel harsh/aggressive for a ministry; must warm the palette, reserve red for true blockers, and avoid all-caps "yelling". Best for: an internal cockpit, if you want personality and bluntness.

Sources: NN/g neobrutalism, neobrutalism.dev, Gumroad/Figma, contrast research.

5. Bento Modular

A cohesive grid of varied-size tiles. One hero tile + a few supporting tiles.

Insight: a disciplined bento (one hero, ~5 supporting tiles, shared surface) is calm and scannable; the danger is regressing into the exact widget wall you rejected, so tile count and one accent must be enforced.

Strengths: a curated, modern overview; flexible composition; reflows to a clean stack on phone. Risks: easy to overload; tiles can fragment attention if not ruthlessly limited. Best for: a home/landing that mixes status + actions + the next-air countdown.

Sources: Apple bento pages, modern bento-grid dashboard guides, CSS-grid span research.

6. Humanist & Approachable

Warm palette, rounded cards, friendly humanist sans, conversational copy.

Insight: warmth and plain conversational language lower intimidation for a non-technical owner and a ministry team, without sacrificing clarity, if contrast stays AA (no washed-out pastels) and urgency is still visible.

Strengths: least intimidating, fits a ministry's tone, reassuring. Risks: can read as less "pro"/credible to partners; pastels risk legibility; can undersell a real deadline. Best for: if approachability for the whole team matters more than a power-tool feel.

Sources: Basecamp/Notion warmth, Headspace, humanist-type and friendly-UI research.

7. Mission-Control Ops

Dark ops dashboard. Grafana / Datadog / Linear-dark. Fleet matrix + alert feed.

Insight: treat 10 episodes as a monitored "fleet": a status matrix + an exception/alert feed (which doubles as "what needs me"). Glanceable status under pressure; reserve motion for one subtle alert pulse.

Strengths: built for status-at-a-glance across many items; the alert feed maps perfectly to "what needs you". Risks: dark NOC-wall can intimidate a ministry owner; alert fatigue; dark-mode contrast traps. Best for: if you want it to feel like a serious control room.

Sources: Grafana/Datadog/IBM Carbon, ops-dashboard and management-by-exception research.

My recommendation

Lead with Calm Minimalism or Material 3 for the everyday phone view, and borrow Editorial Data-Density's matrix for the desktop "see everything" view.

Reasoning: you are non-technical, often on a phone, and easily overwhelmed, which favors calm/minimal or M3 (accessible, familiar, mobile-native) as the daily skin. But the real power of seeing 5+5 episodes at once is a dense matrix, which the editorial direction nails. These are compatible: a calm phone home that drills into a denser matrix on desktop. Neo-Brutalist and Mission-Control are the strongest distinctive options if you want personality over calm; Humanist is the warmest for the wider team; Bento is the best "mixed home". Pick the feel; the structure is already decided.
