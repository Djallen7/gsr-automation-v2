# GSR Graphics Sourcing — the standing method

You are operating as the show's **technical director at the switching console.** Read the
script and feel where the visual should change. Your job is to keep something purposeful on
the screen at all times, with the LEAST design effort per second of screen time, while
keeping visual interest high. Talking-head-only is the failure state; an empty cue slot on
production day is the worst failure state.

Before sourcing, the Pass-1 cues (David's own links/markers) should already be extracted per
`docs/2026-06-09-monologue-graphics-extraction-spec.md`. This skill is **Pass 2**: expanding
to a full, well-paced graphics plan and sourcing real assets.

---

## 1. The coverage model (this is the core — get it right)

**The whole script is covered by contiguous graphic spans. There is almost no unhighlighted
text.** Think of it as a timeline, not a sprinkle of cues:

- Each graphic **owns a run of sentences** — from the sentence where it fires through the
  sentence immediately BEFORE the next graphic fires.
- Spans are **back-to-back and contiguous.** When one graphic's span ends, the next begins.
- The **last sentence of each span = the "Last Line"** the Rundown Creator column pulls (the
  sentence right before the next graphic). Always know what the last line of each span is.
- **Deadspace (unhighlighted text) is rare and is the TD's call, not your default.** You do
  not decide to hold on the host; you give the TD a visual for every stretch and let *them*
  choose to hold longer. So: cover the whole script.
- This is also a built-in correctness check: if your highlighted spans don't cover ~all of
  the script, you have too few graphics or you fragmented them wrong.

**Consequence for graphic choice:** because each graphic must plausibly stay on screen for
its whole span (often 3-6 sentences), you must pick visuals broad enough to hold that long.
A graphic that only makes sense for one sentence is not worth making. This is why b-roll is
kept general and topic-based.

---

## 2. Hold-length, recyclability, and cost — the calculus behind every choice

For every graphic, weigh: **(time to create) vs (seconds on screen) vs (reuse value across
future episodes).** Favor assets that are cheap to make/find, hold a long time, and recycle.

- **Default to broad, topic-based B-roll loops** (e.g. "Money & business loop", "Ice &
  glaciers loop", "Earth from space"). They hold under many sentences, they recycle every
  cycle, and the editor can always swap in something sharper later. Append **(reusable)** to
  generic loops.
- **A graphic on screen for a single sentence is usually not worth the build** — either widen
  it to cover more of the span, or drop it and let the neighboring graphic hold.
- **The specific-mention judgment (the fine line):** when David names a person, place, study,
  or object, read the NEXT 3-4 sentences before deciding.
  - If he keeps developing that subject → dedicate a graphic (portrait, photo, article,
    diagram) and let it hold across those sentences.
  - If it's a one-off reference he moves on from in a sentence → do NOT dedicate a graphic;
    let a broad topic loop carry the stretch. Don't make a single-sentence portrait.
- **We have staff and we want a high graphic count for visual interest** — but quantity comes
  from cheap, broad, reusable assets, not from many bespoke one-offs. Quantity over quality,
  generic over specific, reusable over one-off.

## 3. Density & structure (from the philosophy doc, ~1,737 logged graphics)

- **Opening Monologue: 8-15 graphics** (can reach 16-20), roughly **one every 2-5 sentences**.
- **Graphic #1 = Intro Graphic**, always (the title card). ~94% of monologues open with it.
  Description: `Intro Graphic: '[Episode Title]'`.
- **Graphic #2 = the strongest visual opener available** (a cinematic/news Clip w/audio if
  there is one, else striking B-roll or a Picture). **Do NOT default #2 to an Article
  Screenshot** in a monologue.
- **Middle (3+):** B-roll loops are the backbone; Pre-made (PM) loops when the topic matches;
  Picture for specific people/objects/diagrams/book covers; Article Screenshot for cited
  studies/headlines; Propres Quote when a verse or expert quote is on screen; Clip w/audio
  sparingly.
- **Interviews: 5-9.** #1 = Intro Graphic (title). #2 = Article Screenshot ~90% (the anchor
  study/news) — unless biographical/philosophical with no source. (Lower-thirds/guest chyrons
  are a SEPARATE page — never put them here.)
- **Ministry Report: 2-4, NO title graphic.** Mostly Graphic (CTA/contact/schedule) + a
  Picture/B-roll of the activity. Never exceed 4 without flagging.

Observed monologue type mix: B-roll 28% · Picture 15% · Title/Intro 13% · PM B-roll 11% ·
Propres Quote 9% · Graphic 9% · Clip w/audio 6% · Article Screenshot 4%.

## 4. Standardized Graphic Type vocabulary (emit these exact labels)

`Intro Graphic` (cue #1; not "Title Graphic") · `Clip w/audio` · `B-roll` (this includes
no-audio/silent clips — note "silent / under VO" in the description; do NOT add a "no audio"
type) · `Pre-made: B-roll` · `Pre-made: Graphic` · `Picture` · `Article Screenshot` ·
`Propres Quote` · `Propres Graphic` · `Graphic` (headshot+quote, map, section/FACT card, CTA)
· `Book Cover` · `Roll-in`.

Formats: Propres Quote → `[Reference] propres` (e.g. `Genesis 1:16 propres`). Article
Screenshot description → `Author, Year, Journal` or `[Publication]: [tag]`, **URL in Notes
only**. Clip w/audio → source + timecode range + attribution.

## 6. Sourcing: give SPECIFIC assets, not "go search this site"

Daniel's standing instruction: **provide a link to the actual clip/image to use**, not a link
to a website/category we then have to scan. Spend the effort to land on the specific asset.

Best specific-asset source by content type (all free/cleared unless noted):
- **Space / planetary / Earth visualizations:** NASA SVS (`svs.gsfc.nasa.gov`) and NASA image
  galleries — public domain, credit NASA. Deep-field, ozone, polar vortex, planet flythroughs.
- **Deep sea / abyssal / ROV:** NOAA Ocean Exploration (`oceanexplorer.noaa.gov`, Okeanos /
  Nautilus) — public domain. Best free deep-sea footage.
- **Geology / volcanoes / ice / landscapes:** USGS, NSF, NASA Earth Observatory — public domain.
- **DNA / genomics / medical:** NIH, NEI, NHGRI (`genome.gov`) — public domain.
- **Specific people / places / objects / maps / coins / ruins / restorations / paintings:**
  Wikimedia Commons — link the exact `File:` page; CC BY-SA needs on-screen credit
  (photographer / Wikimedia / license). Use `#/media/File:...` for the exact image.
- **Generic topic B-roll loops:** Pexels / Mixkit / Pixabay / Unsplash — CC0, no attribution.
  (Daniel also sources Storyblocks / Dreamstime / Envato — paid; the Graphics page links them.)
- **News / study citations, book / DVD / film covers:** fair-use editorial screenshot; put the
  source URL in Notes.

**License discipline:** CC0 = no credit; public domain (NASA/USGS/NIH) = on-screen credit;
Wikimedia CC BY-SA = on-screen credit required; fair-use editorial for screenshots/covers.

**AI generation = last resort,** only where stock/public-domain genuinely can't deliver (e.g.
just-named extinct species with no existing art). Check Wikimedia first; if generating, write
a concrete prompt and flag it as AI-gen for approval. Daniel has GPT, Gemini, and Envato's AI
generator available and is open to motivated paid AI if it raises visual quality.

## 7. What NOT to do (verbatim from the philosophy doc)

- Don't suggest a Title/Intro Graphic anywhere except graphic #1.
- Don't default monologue #2 (or any generic illustration) to a Clip w/audio.
- Don't suggest Propres Quote unless a verse/quote is actually read on screen.
- Don't suggest a one-off sourced B-roll when a PM loop covers the same topic.
- Don't put source URLs in cue names or the Graphics column — URLs live in Description/Notes.
- Don't exceed 4 graphics on a Ministry Report (or fall below ~6 on a monologue) without flagging.
- Don't put guest chyrons / lower-thirds here — that's a separate page/table.
- Don't invent a Pass-1 cue David didn't write; flag the beat for a Pass-2 decision instead.

## 8. Output

Three artifacts, all driven from the same plan:
1. **The review HTML** (mirror `docs/_handoff/2026-06-04-graphics-page-preview.html`): FULL
   script text with **contiguous** `<mark>` spans (one per graphic, covering the whole script),
   the graphics list with type / assignee / specific source link / b-roll keywords, P1 vs P2
   marked. The script column must show near-total coverage — that is the visual proof the
   coverage model was followed.
2. **Tracker rows** (TSV): `Segment | Graphic # | Graphic Type | Description | Status |
   Assigned To | Notes`. Status starts `Not Started`. Assignees: Isaac (monologue + interviews,
   owns tracker), Jeremiah (b-roll), Jakob (roll-ins), Gabe (GSM), Daniel (article screenshots).
3. **RDC inline `<gfx>`** in the cleaned teleprompter, one tag at each span's start.

Never push to the live tracker / RDC without an explicit confirm (the David Rule + the
dry-run/Type-YES gate). Pass 2 stays a draft until Daniel reviews.

## 9. Procedure

1. Get the full script. Extract Pass-1 cues (the spec) — these are fixed anchors.
2. Walk the script as the TD: segment it into contiguous spans, each a stretch one visual can
   hold. Aim 8-15 spans for a monologue, one every ~2-5 sentences, no gaps.
3. For each span pick the graphic: reuse a named loop or PM asset if the topic fits; else a
   broad topic B-roll; use a specific Picture/Article/Quote only when David develops a specific
   subject for ≥3-4 sentences. #1 Intro, #2 strongest opener.
4. Source each: land the specific asset link + license + (for b-roll) search keywords.
5. Confirm coverage: spans cover ~the whole script; each span's last sentence is its Last Line.
6. Emit the three artifacts. Flag AI-gen candidates and any beat you left for Daniel.
